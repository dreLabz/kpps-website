Managing Pre-Competition Anxiety: What Actually Works

Dr. Jenny Kraska · January 15, 2025 · 5 min read Performance

Pre-competition anxiety is nearly universal among competitive athletes. The question is never whether you'll feel it — it's what you do with it. Despite decades of research, many athletes and coaches still reach for strategies that either don't work or actively interfere with performance.

This article reviews what the evidence actually supports, distinguishes it from common myths, and offers practical application guidance for athletes and coaches at any level.

Understanding Anxiety in Competitive Settings

Anxiety is a normal physiological and psychological response to perceived threat. In athletic competition, the "threat" is usually evaluation: the possibility of failure, embarrassment, or not meeting expectations. The body responds as it would to any perceived danger — heart rate increases, attention narrows, muscles tense.

This response is not inherently bad. A moderate level of arousal is associated with optimal performance in most sport tasks. The problem arises when anxiety becomes dysregulating — when it consumes attentional resources, undermines confidence, or creates a spiral of catastrophic thinking.

What Doesn't Work (and Why It Persists)

Trying to eliminate anxiety

The instinct to remove anxiety before competition is understandable but counterproductive. Attempts at thought suppression tend to increase the frequency and salience of the unwanted thought — sometimes called the "white bear" problem, after research showing that trying not to think about something makes you think about it more.

Forced positive thinking

Telling yourself "I've got this" when you don't believe it tends to backfire — not because positivity is wrong, but because the brain is good at detecting incongruence. Forcing a positive state when your actual state is anxious creates cognitive dissonance, not calm.

What the Evidence Supports

Reappraisal: interpreting arousal as readiness

One of the most replicated findings in sport psychology involves reappraisal — specifically, reinterpreting pre-competition arousal as a sign of readiness rather than anxiety. Telling yourself "I'm excited" rather than "I'm nervous" is not a denial of your state; it's a recalibration of its meaning. Research by Alison Wood Brooks demonstrated that this simple shift improved performance on anxiety-inducing tasks including public speaking and math.

Controlled breathing

Diaphragmatic breathing with extended exhalation directly activates the parasympathetic nervous system, reducing physiological arousal. A 4-7-8 breath pattern (inhale for 4, hold for 7, exhale for 8) is one well-studied protocol. Consistent practice is required for it to become reliable under competitive pressure.

Acceptance-based approaches

Rather than fighting anxiety, acceptance-based approaches encourage athletes to acknowledge what they're feeling without judgment and redirect attention to the task at hand. This is grounded in Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) principles — research has demonstrated effectiveness in sport performance contexts.

The goal is not to feel calm. The goal is to perform well while feeling whatever you feel.

Pre-performance routines

Structured pre-competition routines reduce decision fatigue and create a reliable attentional anchor. The specific activities matter less than their consistency and the athlete's relationship to them. Effective routines typically include physical activation, mental focus, and a final activation cue timed to performance onset.

Practical Takeaways

  • Expect anxiety. Plan for it rather than trying to eliminate it.
  • Practice your regulation tools in training, not just on competition day.
  • Use reappraisal language: "I'm ready" rather than "I'm not nervous."
  • Develop a consistent pre-competition routine with clear behavioral anchors.
  • Work with a sport psychologist to develop individualized strategies based on your specific performance profile.

If you're finding that performance anxiety is significantly interfering with your competition experience or outcomes, consider reaching out for a consultation. Structured sport psychology work can accelerate the development of reliable mental tools considerably.
